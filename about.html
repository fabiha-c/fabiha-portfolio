Who I Am

I'm Fabiha, a senior at NYU Tandon studying Biomolecular Science with a minor in Public Policy and Management. As a GLASS (Global Leaders and Scholars in STEM) Scholar, I've spent the past three years exploring oral health inequity as a global challenge—shadowing dental providers in Sri Lanka and Kenya, conducting oral microbiome research at NYU Abu Dhabi, and working as a dental assistant in underserved New York communities.

My work focuses on understanding how clinician training and systemic barriers influence treatment decisions, particularly the prioritization of extractions over restorative care in low-resource settings. I'm passionate about culturally competent, disability-inclusive dental care and the intersection of global health policy and clinical practice.

Beyond research and clinical work, I serve as a GLASS Wellness Ambassador, Writing Partner at Tandon's Writing Center, and member of the Tandon Accessibility Team. I'm preparing to apply to dental school with the goal of becoming a clinician who addresses oral health disparities through patient-centered, equitable care.

GLASS Research

Addressing Oral Health Inequity Through Cross-Cultural Clinical Exposure

SDG 3: Good Health SDG 10: Reduced Inequalities Engineering Health

Oral health inequity remains a persistent global challenge shaped by socioeconomic barriers, cultural perceptions of oral care, and clinician decision-making. Through my experiences as a GLASS Scholar shadowing dental providers in Sri Lanka and Kenya, as well as working as a dental assistant in a local New York clinic, I observed a recurring pattern: extraction-based care was often prioritized over restorative procedures, particularly in low-resource settings. These observations sparked my interest in understanding the structural, clinical, and educational determinants that drive such treatment decisions.

This reflective research paper explores how practitioner training—particularly post-dental school workplace-based learning—influences treatment decision-making for restorative versus extraction-based care. Drawing on findings from the National Institute of Health's Oral Health in America 2021 report, I examine how training sufficiency, clinical comfort, resource constraints, and cultural expectations shape the care offered to patients in both high- and low-resource environments.

My three years in GLASS allowed me to engage deeply with this global challenge through multiple lenses: clinical observation, cross-cultural research, academic coursework in public policy and biomolecular science, and hands-on research at NYU Abu Dhabi's oral microbiome lab. I participated in GLASS programming focused on global health equity, served as a Wellness Ambassador, and facilitated discussions on disability-inclusive healthcare—experiences that shaped my understanding of how systemic inequities manifest in clinical spaces. My role as a research assistant at NYU Dentistry and my work in underserved New York communities further deepened my understanding of patient-centered care and clinical decision-making under real-world constraints.

This paper synthesizes scholarly literature on dental workforce training, treatment planning frameworks, and global oral health trends with my reflections on witnessing these disparities firsthand. I analyze how differences in education systems, continuing education access, and financial structures between the U.S. and lower-resource countries contribute to divergent treatment philosophies.

This paper ultimately seeks to provide insight into how global inequities in oral healthcare can be mitigated through improved practitioner training, context-specific continuing education, and targeted policy interventions in alignment with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals—specifically SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-Being) and SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities). By connecting my GLASS experiences to these frameworks, I aim to demonstrate how individual clinical encounters reflect broader patterns of structural injustice and how future clinicians can contribute to more equitable care delivery.

Areas of Excellence & SDGs

Tandon Impact Area: Engineering Health

I focus on the Health Area of Excellence, which is dedicated to engineering healthcare and life sciences, focusing on discovering new treatments, medical devices, and methods to deliver care. It aims to develop assistive technology to increase accessibility and improve global health outcomes, especially for underserved communities.

My mission to advocate for equitable healthcare and dental care resonates deeply with this area. The commitment to discovering new treatments and improving delivery methods aligns with my goal of providing equitable healthcare resources. My focus on reaching underserved communities mirrors the area's emphasis on increasing accessibility and livability for all.

Think of the healthcare system as a complex machine that needs constant improvement to work better for everyone. The "Health" area of excellence is like a team of mechanics and inventors working tirelessly to find new ways to fix and upgrade this machine. My mission is like joining this team with a special focus: I'm not only interested in making the machine work better but ensuring it works well for everyone, especially those who have been ignored or left behind in the past.

UN Sustainable Development Goals

Goal 3: Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages.

This goal focuses on a wide range of aspects crucial for a healthy life, from reducing maternal mortality and preventable diseases to achieving universal health coverage and reducing deaths from pollution. It resonates deeply with my mission to advocate for equitable healthcare and dental care, emphasizing the need for comprehensive, quality health services accessible to all individuals.

Goal 10: Reduce inequality within and among countries.

This goal aims to empower and promote social, economic, and political inclusion for all, regardless of various individual characteristics. It seeks to reduce inequalities of outcome and ensure equal opportunity by eliminating discriminatory practices and promoting appropriate legislation and policies. This aligns with my desire to create a healthcare system that is fair and accessible to all segments of the population.

Oral health inequity directly undermines SDG 3's goal of universal health coverage and access to quality essential healthcare. Despite being integral to overall health, oral care remains excluded from many primary health systems, leaving millions without preventive or restorative services. SDG 10 addresses the inequality gap—oral health disparities are starkly visible across income levels, geographic regions, and cultural contexts.

GLASS has particularly honed my understanding of my personal strengths, encouraging deep self-reflection and enabling me to employ these strengths strategically in both academic and professional contexts. The guidance on effectively utilizing GLASS funds and the insightful tips on creating an impactful professional website have been instrumental in preparing me for future academic pursuits and career opportunities.

What I've enjoyed most about GLASS is the holistic approach it takes towards personal and professional development. Interacting with guest speakers, tackling assignments that challenge me to think critically about my strengths and goals, and the opportunity to research and network in fields that interest me have all been incredibly fulfilling. The chance to collaborate with a diverse cohort, explore new perspectives, and continuously refine my research and leadership skills has made my experience in the GLASS program both enriching and inspiring.
